Integration Plan

A phased initiative to embed computational thinking and scientific inquiry across the primary, middle school, and diploma programmes of an IB international school.

Scope PYP → MYP → DP
Duration 18-month pilot, 3-year rollout
Role Lead designer & coordinator
Context IB International School

This project designed and proposed a cohesive STEM and Computer Science continuum for an IB international school — from early years through to Diploma level. 1. Executive Summary

This proposal outlines a phased plan to design and implement a cohesive Computer Science (CS) and STEM learning continuum across the primary, middle, and diploma programmes of an international school operating within an IB framework. It addresses identified gaps in current STEM integration and provides a clear pathway for developing computational thinking from the early years through to pre-university level.

The initiative aims to:

  • Design and implement an upper-secondary Computer Science pathway serving both IB Diploma and local diploma students, ensuring meaningful choices aligned with student interests and future pathways.
  • Embed STEM inquiry and computational thinking progressively across primary and middle school programmes.
  • Strengthen Maker Space integration as a cross-programme hub for hands-on learning.
  • Build teacher capacity through sustained co-teaching, mentoring, and professional learning.
  • Ensure long-term sustainability through reflection cycles, community engagement, and internal leadership development.
  • Keep student needs, interests, and wellbeing as the central purpose of the initiative at every stage.

This plan is cost-effective and scalable, maximising internal resources while positioning the school as a regional leader in integrated STEM education.

2. Vision and Rationale

2.1 Vision

To establish the school as a model for integrated STEM and Computer Science education within the IB framework, where inquiry, creativity, and problem-solving are cultivated from the early years to the diploma level, with student growth, curiosity, and empowerment as the core drivers.

2.2 Rationale

  • Teacher feedback indicates a need for greater confidence, structured support, and dedicated time to deliver effective STEM-based inquiry.
  • Middle and diploma students lack a clear progression path in computational and design thinking.
  • The Maker Space remains underutilised due to limited staffing, training, and curricular integration.
  • The IB Computer Science DP option has high demand internationally, improving student outcomes and university prospects.
  • A coordinated CS/STEM framework supports the school's strategic vision of innovation, inquiry, and interdisciplinarity.

3. Objectives

  1. Design and implement a flexible upper-secondary Computer Science pathway serving both IB DP and local diploma students.
  2. Integrate STEM and computational thinking progressively across primary and middle school, ensuring conceptual and skill continuity.
  3. Build teacher capacity through co-teaching, modelling, and professional learning that supports inquiry-driven STEM integration.
  4. Coordinate and revitalise the Maker Space as a cross-programme hub for design, technology, and inquiry.
  5. Establish partnerships with local and international universities, STEM organisations, and technology communities.
  6. Develop evaluation and resource systems, including measurable STEM learning indicators and a shared digital resource hub.
  7. Embed a cyclical learning model that ensures key scientific and computational concepts are revisited and deepened over time.

1. Purpose

This document presents a cohesive plan for embedding Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (STEM) across the primary, middle school, and diploma programmes of an international school. It broadens the school's focus from digital literacy to scientific inquiry, experimental exploration, and computational thinking. A cyclical learning model encourages students to revisit and deepen core concepts through recurring stages of discovery, reflection, and creation.

This plan supports the development of upper-secondary STEM pathways — including but not limited to the IB DP Computer Science course and relevant local diploma offerings — ensuring students have multiple options to pursue advanced STEM learning.

2. Guiding Principles

2.1 Design Thinking in a Maker Environment

The school adopts Design Thinking in a Maker Environment as a core pedagogical approach, aligned with the mission to cultivate compassionate, creative, and empowered learners who take meaningful action within an international community. Within a safe, inclusive, and resource-rich maker environment, students:

  • Construct knowledge through tinkering, experimentation, failure, and refinement.
  • Normalise risk-taking and develop resilience and adaptability.
  • View challenges as opportunities for innovation and growth.
  • Leverage diverse perspectives to produce more meaningful and human-centred solutions.

2.2 Equitable and Progressive STEM Access

  1. Equity and inclusion: All students have structured entry points into experimentation and computational thinking.
  2. Science as the core of STEM: Students learn to observe, hypothesise, experiment, and connect patterns before applying technology.
  3. Inquiry-based progression: All learning starts from curiosity, hands-on exploration, and links to authentic contexts.
  4. Cyclical reinforcement: Concepts are revisited at increasing levels of depth.
  5. Teacher empowerment: Teachers are supported through planning, modelling, and co-teaching sessions.
  6. Continuity and coherence: Each programme builds upon the previous one, preparing students for complex reasoning across upper-secondary STEM pathways.
  7. Design Thinking: A Maker Space environment enables students to construct knowledge through iterative creation, collaboration, and reflection.

5.1 Reflection and Documentation

Teachers document reflections through short collaborative journals, supporting peer observation and shared inquiry into classroom practice. Informal mentoring and drop-in sessions are available throughout the year. Reflection templates and examples are shared in the resource hub.

6. Learning Rhythm Model

Building on research into cyclical and experiential learning, this framework applies three stages across all units:

  1. Experience: Engage the senses through hands-on exploration and observation.
  2. Reflection: Retell, illustrate, or discuss the experience, linking it to conceptual understanding.
  3. Return: Revisit the same idea later through a new context, adding complexity or abstraction.

This approach strengthens memory, understanding, and curiosity. It fits naturally within the IB inquiry model, especially in the Tuning In and Going Further stages.

6.1 Assessment Philosophy

Assessment focuses on conceptual understanding, process skills, and reflection rather than isolated outcomes. Students maintain digital portfolios documenting their inquiry cycles, reflections, and design iterations. Assessment aligns with IB criteria across programmes, emphasising conceptual understanding, scientific reasoning, and computational processes.

8. Upper-Secondary CS Documentation

8.1 Course Overview and Aims

The Computer Science course develops computational thinking, analytical reasoning, and systems understanding. Students learn to design, implement, and evaluate computing solutions that address real-world problems, with a strong focus on ethical and scientific contexts.

  • Apply scientific reasoning and computational thinking in solving authentic problems.
  • Develop independent learners who design, test, and evaluate digital systems.
  • Understand the social, environmental, and ethical implications of computing.

8.2 Syllabus Structure

Core topics: system fundamentals; computer organisation; networks; computational thinking and problem solving; case study.

Higher Level extensions: abstract data structures; resource management; control systems and simulations.

8.3 Assessment Outline

  • External assessment: IB written examinations (~70% weighting).
  • Internal assessment: Individual software development project (~30 hours) including problem definition, design, testing, evaluation, and reflection (~30% weighting).
  • Formative assessment: code reviews, lab reports, and portfolio entries documenting inquiry and design cycles.

8.4 Resources and Lab Plan

Software: Java (IB-assessed) and Python (conceptual teaching), VS Code or Replit, GitHub for version control, data analysis libraries, and simulation environments. Hardware: laptops, sensors, and optional microcontrollers. The Maker Space is used for prototyping and hardware-related IA projects.
